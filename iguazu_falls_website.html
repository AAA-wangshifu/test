Discovering Iguazu

A natural masterpiece straddling the border between Brazil and Argentina

Located on the border between the Brazilian state of Paraná and the Argentine province of Misiones, Iguazu Falls stands as one of the most awe-inspiring natural wonders on our planet. The name "Iguazu" originates from the Guarani words "y" (water) and "ûasú" (big), literally translating to "big water" — a description that, while accurate, barely captures the overwhelming grandeur of this UNESCO World Heritage Site (UNESCO, 1984).

The falls system comprises approximately 275 individual cascades spanning nearly 2.7 kilometers in width, with the most dramatic section being the Devil's Throat (Garganta del Diablo), where the river plunges over 80 meters into a narrow chasm, creating a thunderous roar that can be heard from kilometers away (IUCN, 2024). The surrounding Iguazu National Park protects one of the largest remaining fragments of the Atlantic Forest, a biodiversity hotspot that harbors over 2,000 species of vascular plants and iconic wildlife including jaguars, giant otters, and toucans (Chimu Adventures, 2018).

First brought to global attention by Spanish conquistador Álvar Núñez Cabeza de Vaca in 1541, the falls have since evolved from a remote jungle curiosity into one of the world's premier ecotourism destinations, attracting over 3.2 million visitors annually across both the Brazilian and Argentine sides (Visit 7 Wonders, 2025). Yet despite its popularity, Iguazu remains a place of profound natural power — where the eternal rush of water reminds us of nature's untamed majesty.

Core Attractiveness

What makes Iguazu Falls one of the most captivating destinations on Earth

The Devil's Throat

The most dramatic section of the falls, where the Iguazu River converges into a U-shaped chasm, plunging 82 meters with such force that a permanent mist cloud rises 150 meters into the air. This is the heart of Iguazu's power — a place where visitors can stand on suspended walkways and feel the earth tremble beneath their feet (Ripio Turismo, 2024).

Atlantic Forest Biodiversity

The surrounding national parks protect one of the planet's most threatened ecosystems. Home to over 400 bird species, 80 mammals, and countless reptiles and amphibians, the forest offers extraordinary wildlife encounters. The vibrant Toco Toucan, with its oversized orange bill, has become an emblematic symbol of the region's rich avian life (Eco Iguassu, 2024).

Natural Spectacles

The perpetual mist generated by the falling water creates countless rainbows that arch across the gorge, transforming the landscape into a kaleidoscope of color. During full moon nights, the Argentine side offers rare lunar rainbow tours, where visitors can witness moonbows shimmering in the cascade's spray — a magical phenomenon few places on Earth can offer.

Case Study: Sustainable Tourism Development

Balancing conservation with world-class visitor experiences

Iguazu Falls represents a compelling case study in sustainable tourism management, demonstrating how a globally significant natural site can accommodate millions of visitors while preserving ecological integrity. The falls are jointly managed by two national parks — Iguazú National Park in Argentina and Iguaçu National Park in Brazil — which together form a transboundary conservation complex that has become a model for international cooperation in protected area management (IUCN, 2024).

The tourism trajectory of Iguazu underwent a dramatic transformation following its designation as one of the New Seven Wonders of Nature in 2011. Visitor numbers surged from approximately 900,000 annually to over 3.2 million by 2024, with the Brazilian side alone recording 1.89 million visitors in 2024 — the highest post-pandemic figure (Visit 7 Wonders, 2025). This influx brought both economic prosperity and significant environmental pressures, necessitating innovative management strategies.

The Public Use Management Plan (2021)

In response to escalating visitor pressure, the Argentine National Parks Administration (APN) implemented a comprehensive Public Use Management Plan in 2021, establishing a daily carrying capacity of 6,902 visitors. The plan introduced timed entry systems, expanded interpretive infrastructure along National Route 101 to decompress the waterfall area, and implemented the SMART surveillance system for monitoring poaching and illegal fishing (IUCN, 2024).

On the Brazilian side, concessionaire Urbia Cataratas has pioneered several sustainability initiatives that have garnered international recognition. The company has invested 3.6 million BRL in the Onças do Iguaçu (Jaguars of Iguazu) Project, a flagship conservation program aimed at protecting the region's apex predator population (Global Vision Access, 2025). Additionally, the park has implemented water reuse systems using treated effluents for toilet flushing, significantly reducing freshwater consumption. Free potable water stations have been installed throughout the park to eliminate single-use plastic bottles, while over 3,000 native trees have been planted as part of reforestation efforts.

The economic model of Iguazu demonstrates how conservation can finance itself. Entrance fees and tourism services generate sufficient revenue to cover park management, research, and conservation activities, with surplus funds supporting the broader National Parks Authority (IUCN, 2024). This financial sustainability has enabled continuous investment in infrastructure, ranger training, and ecological monitoring programs. The park's 2017-2023 Management Plan was developed through broad stakeholder participation, including local communities and indigenous Guarani peoples, ensuring that conservation strategies reflect diverse perspectives and needs.

However, significant challenges persist. The Baixo Iguaçu hydroelectric plant upstream poses ongoing threats to aquatic biodiversity and natural water flow patterns. Climate change has intensified extreme weather events, with extraordinary floods and droughts becoming increasingly common (IUCN, 2024). Invasive species, including 123 non-native plant species, continue to threaten native ecosystems. Furthermore, the concentration of visitors during peak periods — when daily numbers can exceed 8,000 — strains infrastructure capacity and creates crowding that diminishes the visitor experience.

Looking forward, the future of Iguazu as a sustainable tourism destination depends on several critical factors. The expansion of low-impact tourism offerings, such as silent kayaking on secondary rivers and ethnobotanical walks led by Guarani communities, provides a pathway for diversifying the visitor experience while reducing pressure on the main waterfall area (Biosphere Sustainable, 2025). The Tri-National Corridor initiative, involving Brazil, Argentina, and Paraguay, aims to create a contiguous protected landscape that enhances habitat connectivity for wide-ranging species like jaguars. As the global tourism industry increasingly prioritizes sustainability, Iguazu's integrated approach to conservation, community engagement, and visitor management offers valuable lessons for other World Heritage sites facing similar pressures.
